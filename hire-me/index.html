I'll often work with in-house teams on agile projects that need a round of rapid prototyping followed by production-quality code. I prefer to be ingrained with a team, working in the same room as them, rather than working off-site. Where that's not possible (such as if a client is based abroad), I'll arrange a daily standup with the team to feed back what I've worked on that day, ask any questions, and make sure things are on track.

I work closely with the designers and back-end developers to ensure the project's vision is maintained through the different work streams. When I work directly with the designer, they'll often only need to give me very low-fidelity wireframes (usually just a series of rectangles and scribbles on a piece of paper), skipping the photoshop phase. I can quickly turn these into functioning prototypes which I iterate on with the designer.

While I'm happy to pitch in with some of the design work alongside a designer and have a decent eye for typography, I am not a designer and I won't be comfortable working on aspects such as mockups or iconography. A lot of people assume I'm a designer, which I find flattering, but I'm absolutely not one!

When I work with an agency, I expect full autonomy with the client, and I don't agree with whitelabelling my services. It doesn't feel right to me and I'm not very good at pretending.

I'm confident using Git (and GitHub) for version control, build systems including Gulp and Grunt, project planning tools such as Jira and Trello, and templating systems such as Jekyll.

As well as working with clients on websites, I also do tech editing for publications. I'm on the team at A List Apart and 24 ways as a tech editor, and I was Ethan Marcotte's tech editor for the second edition of his book Responsive Web Design. I'm currently tech-editing two titles that have yet to be released.

I tech edit work that's still in the outline phase, and also work that's nearly ready to publish.

When I'm sent a piece to tech edit, the main thing I do is check that what's being said is correct to the best of my knowledge. I'll test any code in different browsers, and also add comments where I think something needs to be explained more fully.

Unlike a copy editor, I won't correct spelling or gramatical mistakes, nor will I do line-edits, but I'll often act as a second pair of eyes and flag things that I want the copy editor to look at.
